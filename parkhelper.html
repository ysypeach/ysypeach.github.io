We've all witnessed the success of companies such as Airbnb, Uber, and TaskRabbit. The sharing economy is estimated to grow from $14 billion in 2014 to $335 billion by 2025. Against the background of this growing trend, ParkHelper is a project which explores the concept of "value". We designed a mobile app which connects drivers with property owners who have a parking space they might want to share. The question we asked ourselves: what sort of value can we generate when community and culture are brought into the design conscious?

Client: Self-Initiated Project
Duration: One Week, 2017
Team: Angel Yu, Joy Chen, Justin Fanzo, MARC ESTRUCH TENA
My Role: End-to-end UX Design, Visual Design, Branding

Design process

Planning for
Strategic Research

This project challenged us to create value through an exchange of resources between drivers and property owners. Given we had only a week, the team kicked off with an ecosystem brain dump, which revealed what we knew and what we needed to learn. Our questions focused around four major areas: parking behaviors, parking pain points, attitude towards money, and appeal of a mobile app. We conducted 5 interviews with property owners and 7 interviews with drivers by randomly approaching pedestrians on the street.

Top Insights

People prefer long-term parking arrangements to avoid hassle

Money is a nice-to-have income for property owners, but a priority for drivers 

People can create unexpected value for each other

Design Decision:
Long Term Parking to Save Cost

Out of our insights a concept started to emerge: we decided to create a mobile app which allows for drivers to reserve long term parking spots from property owners who have an available space. Some key requirements that came up in our research: the app needed to offer a cheaper alternative to street parking, provide a means of reassurance for users, and make parking convenient. Since we were focusing on long term usage, we saw that to be an important design asset. We started to brainstorm other values that could be generated from a long-term relationship.

The Bigger Picture:
Values Beyond the Monetary

The answer, we found, lies in the quest itself. It's difficult to imagine designing for something intangible, as they are often elusive to identification. We found the answer in a stranger society, where people have lived in a place for years without knowing their next-door neighbor. What if we could promote a culture that supports values of connection, trust, and friendliness? We saw the app as a medium, which connects to different layers of "value": the monetary, the personal and societal.

Storyboarding & Usage Flow

We created 20 storyboards to explore possible ways this concept might take shape. In the final user flow, Rebecca, who is a driver, searches for a parking spot. She chooses one of the options presented, and puts forward a request for reservation. Madeline, who is the property owner, receives a notification on her mobile. She looks at Rebecca's profile to learn about her background, and decides to approve the request. Rebecca now can enjoy the parking spot at a low cost as an alternative to more expansive options. She also has the option to accept a help request, which is posted by members of the ParkHelper community. If she accepts and completes the task, the app rewards her parking points which can be used towards her parking payment or bank account.

On Facilitating Value Through
Help Request

A important design decision was whether to make help tasks mandatory. In our first round of design, we made it mandatory for drivers to perform a daily task in return for a reduced parking price. However, this turned out to be too stressful. In our later iterations, we made these tasks optional. 

Final Thoughts

Some of the challenging questions of this project include: How do you transform parking from a mundane hassle to an experience with meaningful layers?  How do you facilitate interactions in a way that is non-obtrusive? How does design traverse fluidly between tangible and intangible subjects? These are some of the harder questions of design. As products expand, finding these invisible threads and bigger questions will be crucial.
